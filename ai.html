Reflection on "With 'AI slop' distorting our reality, the world is sleepwalking into disaster", Nesrine Malik

MEDPL150 • Spring 2026

Reflection

In the words of Nesrine Malik, "Profound Disorientation" is exactly how I'd describe my experience with the integration of AI-generated content in everyday life. It now exists beyond just social media and technology; I must stop myself on the street and wonder whether the movie poster or advertisement was not made by AI, but generated. Generated is the word we use, and it's fitting, as the use of "made" would imply that hard work and effort were put into it. We don't — shouldn't — think of AI work as something that was made, regardless of the human effort it may have taken to code and train the algorithm. Once the platform or product reaches mainstream media and evidently the hands of the public, it gets warped and molded to the desired reality of whoever, and it really is whoever, can get their hands on it.

Of course, I believe that AI generation can have a positive outcome. I've seen TikToks of people "bringing back" their loved ones through AI-generated photos or videos, and didn't completely hate the use of the artificial platform, but a part of me knew that I could never see myself going down that road, a road I truly believe shouldn't even be open to us. Malik says, "The overall effect of being exposed to AI images all the time, from the nonsensical to the soothing to the ideological, is that everything begins to land in a different way." His words could not be more fitting, echoing what I said about questioning everyday productions. We choose AI because it can give us what we want, whenever we want. We can craft our social algorithm to perfection so that we never have to see our dislikes, our oppositions, or criticism. This social bias then affects decision making and bleeds into every aspect of life.

Whether it be a simple homework question to ChatGPT, or a prompt into CanvaAI, creation is a touch away for us all. But this easy, quick generation is exactly why we forget to think for ourselves. Children who have not existed in an age before mainstream AI know that they always have the option and that it has always existed. AI, I accept, makes our lives easier, but unnecessarily so. In fact, if you don't want to write an essay for your English class, why are you even here? This is not to eliminate the use of it completely; it's an important step in world evolution, but to caution restraint. "AI Slop" becoming a mainstream mantra is already a step in the right direction, highlighting the importance of calling out artificial art that seeks to take the place of true art. Malik's comments about the dangers of AI and media curation are only the beginning, and many have already started to try to break free of the "algorithm" and the clutches of META, the supposedly main culprit of social media addiction.

Easier access to AI and mass generation of AI art leads to deeper immersion into "subjective worlds rather than objective reality", which makes dystopian movies seem much closer than we thought before. Malik's analysis of the direct effect of AI generated content on media literacy strengthened my existing belief that AI needs rules and regulations to stop the spread of misinformation, mass panic, and other public crises that cause society to deteriorate further.
